Overview

Dow Theory is one of the earliest and most important frameworks in technical analysis. It was derived from the writings of Charles Dow, co-founder of The Wall Street Journal and creator of the Dow Jones market averages. Dow's goal was to measure the overall health of the economy through stock market behavior. He concluded that stock prices move in identifiable trends and reflect economic expectations.

Dow Theory explains:

  • How market trends form
  • How trends are confirmed
  • How the stock market anticipates economic events
  • The role of market averages and volume in trend confirmation

Even today, Dow Theory remains influential because many modern indicators rely on its principles, especially the concept of confirmation.

KEY Concepts

1. Market Prices Discount Everything

The stock market acts as a discounting mechanism.

  • Prices reflect all known information and expectations.
  • The market typically anticipates events at least three months into the future.
  • Rising prices suggest positive expectations.
  • Falling prices suggest negative expectations.

2. Two Market Averages Must Be Used

Dow used two key indexes to analyze market trends:

  1. Dow Jones Industrial Average (DJIA) - Represents companies producing goods.
  2. Dow Jones Transportation Average (DJTA) - Originally the Railroad Average. Represents companies transporting goods.

Dow believed the economy requires production and distribution, so both averages must move together to confirm economic strength.

3. Closing Prices Are Most Important

Dow Theory emphasizes:

  • Only closing prices should be analyzed.
  • Closing prices represent the most important price of the trading day.

4. Confirmation is Essential

The most important principle in Dow Theory is confirmation. A trend is confirmed when both market averages (DJIA & DJTA) move in the same direction.

Confirmed Uptrend:

  • Both averages close above previous secondary highs

Confirmed Downtrend:

  • Both averages close below previous secondary lows

Confirmation does not need to occur on the same day, but should occur within a reasonable time period.

Figure 1.0 - Example of Dow Theory Confirmation

This chart illustrates the Dow Theory principle of confirmation:

  • Top Chart: Dow Jones Industrial Average (DJIA)
  • Bottom Chart: Dow Jones Transportation Average (DJTA)

Both averages formed a major top and bottom around the same period, showing synchronized market movement. According to Dow Theory, a trend is considered reliable only when both averages move in the same direction. If one index fails to confirm the other, it creates a non-confirmation warning signal.

5. Non-Confirmation Signals Potential Trend Reversal

When one average makes a new high or low but the other does not:

  • It is called non-confirmation
  • It signals a possible change in trend

Figure 1.1 - Example of Dow Theory Non-Confirmation

Figure 1.1 shows a Dow Theory non-confirmation where the Dow Jones Industrial Average (DJIA) formed a higher high while the Dow Jones Transportation Average (DJTA) formed a lower high, signaling weakening market participation and warning of a potential reversal in the prevailing uptrend.

6. Markets Move in Three Trends

Dow Theory states that three trends occur simultaneously in the market.

Primary Trend

  • Long-term market movement
  • Duration: 1 year or more
  • Represents bull or bear market

Secondary Trend

  • Intermediate correction within primary trend
  • Duration: weeks to several months

Minor Trend

  • Short-term fluctuations
  • Duration: days

Figure 1.2 - Dow Theory: Primary, Secondary, and Minor Trends

Figure 1.2 - NVIDIA's weekly chart illustrates the Dow Theory concept that markets move in three simultaneous trends. From October 2022 to June 2024, NVIDIA rallied from around 11 levels to 140 levels, representing a strong primary uptrend (bull market). During this larger upward move, the stock experienced several intermediate pullbacks and consolidations, which represent secondary trends or corrections within the main trend. Short-term day-to-day price fluctuations occurring within these movements are known as minor trends.

7. Volume Confirms the Trend

Volume acts as secondary confirmation.

  • Volume should increase in the direction of the prevailing trend.
  • Rising prices with rising volume confirm bullish strength.
  • Falling prices with rising volume confirm bearish strength.

Figure 1.3 - Volume Confirmation of Trend

NVIDIA's weekly chart demonstrates the Dow Theory principle that volume should confirm the prevailing trend. During the strong primary uptrend from 2022 to 2024, rising prices were generally accompanied by increasing volume, confirming bullish strength. Conversely, during secondary corrections and pullbacks, declining prices were often associated with lower volume, indicating temporary weakness rather than a major trend reversal.

Why Charles Dow Created Market Averages?

In the late 19th century, economists did not have reliable tools to measure the health of the U.S. economy. Charles Dow therefore created stock market averages to serve as an economic barometer.

Railroad Average (1884)

  • Included 11 companies
  • Mostly railroad stocks

Later Dow realized railroads transported goods produced by industrial companies.

Dow Jones Industrial Average (1896)

  • Represented industrial production

Dow believed: If goods are produced but not transported, economic growth is not real. Therefore, industrial and transportation averages must confirm each other.

Evolution of Dow Theory

Dow did not formally create Dow Theory. Later analysts developed it further.
